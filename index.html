Microscopic Imaging

How can microscopic imaging benefit Alzheimer's research?

Microscopic imaging has played a central role in neurodegenerative disease research for decades, particularly in Alzheimer’s disease. Different microscopy techniques offer distinct advantages and limitations, and selecting the appropriate imaging strategy can greatly accelerate experimental discovery. In this article, I review the major microscopy approaches currently used in Alzheimer’s research and summarize their key strengths and limitations.

Alzheimer's Disease

Can molecular light degrade amyloid misfolded proteins?

Misfolded protein accumulation is a central pathological feature of Alzheimer’s disease. However, selectively removing them remains a major challenge. In this study, we explored a new strategy using ADLumin-5, a self-photosensitizing chemiluminescent compound, to generate molecular light and induce targeted photo-oxidation and photodegradation of misfolded proteins. We demonstrated that ADLumin-5 could promote the degradation of several disease-associated proteins, including beta-amyloid, tau, alpha-synuclein, and TDP-43. This work suggests that molecular light–induced protein degradation may provide a new photo-theranostic strategy for both imaging and treating Alzheimer's disease.

Glymphatic system

Does the brain also have a structural lymphatic vessel network?

For decades, the brain was thought to lack a conventional lymphatic vessel network. The glymphatic system, first described by Dr. Maiken Nedergaard’s group, offered a new model: cerebrospinal fluid enters along periarterial spaces, flows through the brain, clears waste, and exits along perivenous routes. Yet one major question remains: how does CSF move so rapidly through the brain? Using CRANAD3, an amyloid-binding fluorescent probe, we unexpectedly discovered extensive nanoscale lymphatic-like structures in brain tissue. This observation suggests that the brain may contain a previously unrecognized structural network that helps explain rapid CSF transport and waste clearance.

Membrane Protein Dynamics

How does the axon initial segment regulate protein diffusion and plasticity?

The axon initial segment (AIS) is a highly specialized and crowded region at the beginning of the axon, where action potentials are initiated. Its unique structural organization helps maintain neuronal polarity, control excitability, and regulate protein mobility within neurons. Studying the AIS can provide important insights into neuronal function and disease-related vulnerability. In this project, I measured the diffusion dynamics of SK transmembrane proteins at the AIS and in other neuronal compartments. I also examined how F-actin organization, especially the periodic ring structure, changes in response to drug and toxin treatments.

Membrane mechanics

Can axon protect itself from mechanical stress?

The axon plasma membrane skeleton composes of periodic actin rings connected by spectrin filaments and it provides structural support for axonal stability. How this network responds to mechanical stretch remains unclear. In this work, we developed a coarse-grained molecular dynamics model to study its behavior during extension and relaxation. Our simulation results showed that spectrin unfolding helps buffer mechanical tension and protect the axon from structural collapse, while excessive stretch or weakened actin–spectrin interactions can lead to junction dissociation and network rupture. This study helps explain how cytoskeleton contributes to axonal resilience under mechanical stress.
